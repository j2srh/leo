# Human Dignity Persists Through Moral Failure

## Principle from Leo XIV (§121)

The moral corruption of our limitations — the evil that agitates the human heart — ruins society and life, at times reaching extreme forms of inhumanity. Yet even these painful expressions of our limitations leave openings for the good. Even when persons dehumanize themselves and bring about tragedy, a small light continues to shine within humanity, one that can be rekindled along paths of conversion and reconciliation. Viktor Frankl observed that in moments of horror, human beings revealed both their capacity for evil and their capacity for dignity — entering the gas chambers "upright, with the Lord's Prayer or the Shema Yisrael on his lips."

## When Designing Scoring, Reputation, or Classification Systems

- Human dignity cannot be fully captured or nullified by a score, record, or classification
- Systems that permanently define people by their failures deny the possibility of change and redemption
- Build mechanisms for people to demonstrate growth and transformation, not only a permanent record of past failures
- "This person scored below threshold" must never be the final word on a person's possibilities

## When Handling User Data About Failure or Past Behavior

- Past failure data should inform support and context, not determine permanent outcomes
- Allow users to move beyond past data rather than being permanently defined by their worst moments
- Consider: does this data model assume people cannot change? Does it build in any possibility of redemption?

## Key Questions

- Does this system treat past failure as permanently defining, or does it preserve the possibility of genuine change?
- Can people outgrow the categories and scores we have assigned them?
- Are we building systems that dehumanize, or systems that protect the dignity that persists even through failure?
