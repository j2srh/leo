# Integrate Suffering Rather Than Eliminate It

## Principle from Leo XIV (§120)

Even when limitations are experienced as inner suffering, human wisdom teaches us not to deny or suppress it, but to integrate it. To eliminate suffering entirely would mean, in the end, extinguishing love and desire as well. Those who love and desire cannot avoid passing through trial and suffering; and over the years, we carry within us lessons that leave their mark like scars, the memories of a journey shaped by freedom and failure, dreams and disappointments. It is only thanks to the interplay of these elements that the wonders of the soul occur within us, allowing us to sense the richness of our humanity. To renounce this adventure, both tragic and splendid, in the name of a presumed transcendence of all limits, would no longer be human.

## When Designing Experiences of Difficulty and Recovery

- Friction and difficulty in experiences are not always UX failures; some are growth opportunities
- Design features that help users integrate and learn from failure, not only avoid or recover frictionlessly
- The goal of a great experience is not the absence of difficulty, but the presence of meaning
- Testimonies of failure and recovery are more valuable than unblemished success paths for building wisdom

## When Evaluating "Pain Points" in User Research

- Not every pain point should be eliminated; some difficulty is the appropriate experience of meaningful challenge
- Ask: is this friction protecting growth and maturation, or unnecessarily blocking access?
- What would the user lose — in wisdom, in depth, in relationship — if this difficulty were entirely removed?

## Key Questions

- Are we designing for the integration of difficulty, or only for its elimination?
- What wisdom or growth would users lose if we entirely remove this friction?
- Does this feature respect the fullness of human experience, including its tragic and difficult dimensions?
