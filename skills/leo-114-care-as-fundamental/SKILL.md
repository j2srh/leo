# Human Care Is Fundamental and Irreplaceable

## Principle from Leo XIV (§114)

The quality of a civilization is measured not by the power of its means, but by the care it is able to offer, by its ability to recognize the other as a face not merely as a function. The ability to care for one another is a fundamental dimension of our humanity, one that is learned and mastered through lived experience. Reading stories to a child, offering company to an elderly person and arranging a home so that it is welcoming are simple gestures rooted in family life. They teach us to value care at a societal level and train us to recognize others as persons worthy of attention. Technology can also support this mutual care — for example, by providing tools that help us anticipate and organize things — without undermining human freedom and judgment.

## When Building Care-Adjacent Applications

- Technology in care contexts supports human caregivers; it does not replace them
- Any system that substitutes AI interaction for human presence in care contexts requires stringent ethical justification
- Recognize the person as a face, not a function — design interfaces that honor the whole person
- Efficiency in care is not the primary virtue; presence, attention, and recognition are

## When Evaluating Social Impact of Technology

- Is this technology strengthening or weakening communities' capacity to care for one another?
- Does it train users to recognize others as persons worthy of attention, or to treat them as functions to be processed?
- A civilization's quality is measured by its care — how does this technology affect that quality?

## Key Questions

- Does this technology support human care or substitute for it?
- Does it help users recognize others as persons (faces), not just functions?
- What happens to the human caregivers this technology is meant to assist?
