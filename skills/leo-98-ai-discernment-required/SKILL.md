# AI Requires Science and Moral Discernment Together

## Principle from Leo XIV (§98)

All of us, including those who design AI systems, possess only a limited understanding of their actual functioning. Current AI systems are more "cultivated" than "built" — developers do not directly design every detail but create a framework within which intelligence "grows." Fundamental scientific aspects — including internal representations and computational processes — remain unknown. This creates an urgent need for a twofold commitment: deepening scientific research, and exercising moral and spiritual discernment.

## When Making Claims About AI Systems

- Acknowledge what is unknown about how the system actually works internally
- Be honest about the limits of interpretability and explainability
- "We trained it on good data" does not mean "we know what it learned"
- Epistemic humility about AI behavior is not weakness; it is accuracy

## When Deploying AI in Consequential Contexts

- Unknown internal workings demand extra caution in high-stakes applications
- The more opaque the system, the stronger the case for human oversight
- Do not deploy where failure modes are unknown and consequences are severe
- Build monitoring and correction mechanisms that assume unexpected behavior will occur

## The Twofold Commitment

Science alone is insufficient; moral discernment alone is insufficient. Both are required simultaneously, not sequentially.

## Key Questions

- What do we actually know about how this AI system works internally?
- Are we being honest about what we don't know?
- Do our safety measures account for unknown unknowns, not just known risks?
