# Build and Defend Just Institutions

## Principle from Leo XIV (§123)

History does not appear solely as a record of human violence, but also as evidence that humanity is capable of creating institutions that protect our shared life. The founding of the International Committee of the Red Cross (1863), the long process leading to the abolition of slavery, the establishment of the United Nations (1945) and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), the 1951 Refugee Convention — in each case, the desire for good took concrete shape in laws, institutions and practices capable of limiting the abuse of power and defending the vulnerable. Yet none of these developments emerged without encountering resistance, narrow interests or cultural inertia. The very fragility of these achievements highlights how precious the responsibility is of those who initiate and sustain them.

## When Working on Technology Policy and Governance

- Good governance and regulation are achievements of moral progress, not obstacles to innovation
- Advocate for AI governance institutions with the same moral seriousness the Red Cross's founding deserves
- Fragile institutions need active defense from those who benefit from their absence
- Resistance and narrow interests will oppose every effort to protect the vulnerable through law — this is normal, not a reason to give up

## When Participating in Standards and Policy Processes

- Show up; these processes are where the protection of the vulnerable is built or abandoned
- The slow development of norms is part of how moral progress happens — do not demand instant results
- Every AI governance standard either protects the vulnerable or leaves them exposed

## Key Questions

- Are we participating in the institutions that protect the vulnerable in the AI era?
- Are we defending fragile governance achievements against those who benefit from their erosion?
- What institutions need to be built or strengthened to protect human dignity in the age of AI?
