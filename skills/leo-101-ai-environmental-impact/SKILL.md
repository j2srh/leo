# AI's Environmental Cost Must Be Counted

## Principle from Leo XIV (§101)

AI is now embedded in decision-making processes across many sectors. Rapidly and uncritically adopting AI exposes us to risks including the tendency to overlook the environmental impact. Current AI systems require enormous amounts of energy and water, significantly influencing carbon dioxide emissions, and place heavy demands on natural resources. As complexity increases, especially in large language models, computing power and storage requirements grow, demanding extensive networks of machines, cables, data centers and energy-intensive infrastructure. It is essential to develop more sustainable technological solutions that reduce environmental impact and help protect our common home.

## When Choosing AI Models or Infrastructure

- Larger models are not automatically better; consider environmental cost vs. task requirements
- Use the smallest model that adequately performs the task
- Consider regional energy sources: AI running on renewable energy has a different environmental footprint
- Batch processing is more efficient than real-time processing; use appropriately
- Cache aggressively to avoid redundant computation

## When Evaluating AI Product Decisions

- Include environmental impact in the cost-benefit analysis of AI features
- "More AI" is not inherently better; marginal AI value must justify marginal environmental cost
- Support development of more energy-efficient AI architectures and training methods
- Transparency about environmental costs is part of honest product communication

## Key Questions

- What is the environmental cost of this AI feature or system?
- Are we choosing the most efficient AI approach for this task?
- Are we factoring environmental impact into our definition of responsible AI use?
