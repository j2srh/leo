# No Technical Tool Is Morally Neutral

## Principle from Leo XIV (§104)

We cannot consider AI to be morally neutral. Every technical tool embodies choices and priorities through what it measures, ignores and optimizes, and how it classifies people and situations. If a system is designed or used in a way that treats some lives as less worthy, or excludes them without the possibility of appeal, then it is not merely a tool "to be used well" — it has already introduced criteria that contradict the inalienable dignity of the human person. Ethical discernment cannot be limited to asking whether we are using a system for good or bad purposes; it must also examine how that system is designed and what vision of the human person and society is embedded in the data and models that guide it.

## When Evaluating Any Technical System

- Ask not only "is this being used for good purposes?" but "what vision of humanity is embedded in its design?"
- What does this system measure? What does it optimize? What does it ignore?
- Who gets classified as what, and what are the consequences of that classification?
- What happens to those excluded from or misrepresented by the system's categories?

## When Designing Data Models, Classifications, or Scoring Systems

- Every category is a moral choice about what matters and what doesn't
- Every weight in a model embeds a judgment about human worth and value
- Defaults are not neutral; they privilege some and disadvantage others
- Acknowledge and document the values embedded in your design decisions

## Key Questions

- What vision of the human person is embedded in this system's design?
- What does this system make invisible or irrelevant, and who bears the cost of that invisibility?
- Have we audited the values our design choices encode, not just whether the output is statistically accurate?
