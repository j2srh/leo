# Human Limits Are Not Defects

## Principle from Leo XIV (§118)

Our relationship with life seems to be in crisis. Everything that appears as a "limit" — incapacity, illness, old age, suffering, vulnerability — tends to be seen primarily as a defect to be corrected, rather than as a reality through which our humanity matures and opens itself to relationship. We must remember that humanity flourishes not despite limitations, but often through them. While it is right to strive to alleviate suffering that marks human life, it is also wise to acknowledge our fundamental finitude — that "religious experience, and in particular Christian faith, propose that we live this ambivalence between human greatness and limitation, interpreting it in the light of our fundamental relationship with God."

## When Designing for Accessibility, Health, or Aging

- Users experiencing limitation are not defective versions of "normal" users — they represent the full range of humanity
- Design that integrates limitation into the human experience is more humane than design that "fixes" users
- The goal is to reduce unnecessary suffering while preserving the dignity and meaning found in limitation
- Vulnerability and dependency are not failures; they are dimensions of human relationship and care

## When Framing Product Narratives

- Avoid framing that positions human limitation as a problem your product "solves"
- Users with chronic illness, disability, or age-related limitations deserve technology built for them, not technology designed to eliminate their difference
- What do your product assumptions say about which human states are acceptable and which are not?

## Key Questions

- Does this design treat human limitation as a defect or as part of the full human experience?
- Are we building for the full range of human states, including vulnerability and dependence?
- What would it mean to design technology that helps people flourish through their limitations, not merely despite them?
