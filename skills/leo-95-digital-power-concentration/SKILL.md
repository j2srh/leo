# Challenge Concentrated Digital Power

## Principle from Leo XIV (§95)

In many cases within the digital context, control over platforms, infrastructure, data and computing power does not rest with States, but with major economic and technological actors. These entities effectively set the conditions for access, determine the rules of visibility and shape the very possibilities for participation. When such power is concentrated in the hands of a few, it tends to become opaque and evade public oversight, increasing the risk of distorted forms of development that give rise to new dependencies, exclusions, manipulations and inequalities.

## When Designing Systems with Centralized Control

- Centralized architectures create concentration of power — consider how this power will be governed
- Who can change the rules of this platform? Who can be excluded, and by whom?
- Are there checks on the platform's power, or does it self-regulate?
- Does this system create new dependencies that users cannot escape?

## When Evaluating Vendor or Platform Choices

- What power does this vendor have over our operations and our users?
- Can we exit if the vendor's interests diverge from our users' interests?
- Is the pricing and access model transparent and fair, or opaque and extractive?
- Does using this platform make us complicit in its power over others?

## Key Questions

- Who controls this platform, and what oversight exists on that control?
- Does this architecture create or reinforce dangerous power concentration?
- Are the rules of participation visible, fair, and subject to legitimate challenge?
