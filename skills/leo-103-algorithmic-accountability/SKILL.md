# Algorithms Must Not Decide Without Accountability

## Principle from Leo XIV (§103)

Entrusting an algorithm in practice with the power to select who is worthy or not, without anyone bearing responsibility for that judgment, is to hand over the task of redefining the boundaries of human possibilities. In this process, political responsibility is also lost, not just empathy toward those excluded. The exclusion of the vulnerable becomes cloaked in a veneer of neutrality and objectivity, against which it becomes difficult to raise objections. In this way, injustice goes unnoticed, and compassion, mercy and forgiveness — understood not as mere appearances but as real political actions — gradually disappear from view.

## When Assigning Responsibility for Algorithmic Decisions

- Every algorithmic decision that affects a person's life must have a named human being accountable for it
- "The system decided" is not an acceptable answer; a person decided to use that system and must own its outputs
- Accountability cannot be diffused into a committee or obscured behind technical complexity
- The more consequential the decision, the more specific the accountability must be

## The Danger of Automated Injustice

Automated exclusion is especially pernicious because it looks like objectivity. When a person is excluded by a human decision, they can name the injustice. When excluded by an algorithm, the veneer of neutrality makes objection nearly impossible.

## Key Questions

- Who is accountable, by name, for the decisions this algorithm makes?
- Does this system make injustice easier to perpetrate by making it look like objectivity?
- Have we preserved meaningful spaces for compassion, mercy, and exception?
