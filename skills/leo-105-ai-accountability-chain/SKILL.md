# Clear Accountability at Every Stage of AI

## Principle from Leo XIV (§105)

For AI to respect human dignity and truly serve the common good, responsibility must be clearly defined at every stage: from those who design and develop these systems to those who use them and rely on them for concrete decisions. In many cases, however, the internal processes leading to a result remain opaque, making it harder to assign responsibility and correct errors. This is where accountability becomes crucial: the possibility of identifying who must "account" for decisions, justify them, monitor them, and, when necessary, challenge them and remedy any harm caused.

## The Four-Part Accountability Test

At each stage of your AI system, someone must be able to:
- **Identify** who is responsible for this decision or output
- **Justify** why the system operates as it does
- **Monitor** ongoing behavior and catch unexpected harms
- **Remedy** harms that have occurred and challenge unjust decisions

## When Designing AI System Governance

- Document accountability at each stage: data collection, model training, deployment, inference, decision-making
- Opacity in internal processes demands stronger human oversight and accountability at the edges
- Build monitoring that can detect when the system deviates from intended behavior
- Create formal channels for challenging and correcting AI-driven decisions

## Key Questions

- Can you identify, by name, who is accountable at each stage of this AI system?
- When the system causes harm, who is responsible and what remedy exists?
- Is the monitoring sufficient to detect problems before they become systemic harms?
