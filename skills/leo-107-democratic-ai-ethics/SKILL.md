# AI Ethics Requires Democratic Governance, Not Just Alignment

## Principle from Leo XIV (§107)

We cannot be satisfied with merely calling for the moralization of machines — the so-called "alignment" of AI with human values — without also having the courage to insist on a further condition: the possibility of openly discussing the ethical frameworks involved and subjecting them to shared standards of social justice. Otherwise, those who control AI will impose their own moral vision, which will become the invisible infrastructure of these systems. A more moral AI is not enough if that morality is determined by a few. What is needed is more active political involvement capable of slowing things down when everything is accelerating, and of protecting the opportunities for communities still to be able to participate and ask questions.

## When Evaluating AI "Alignment" or Ethics Programs

- Who defines the values that the AI is "aligned" with?
- Are those value choices subject to open public debate, or privately determined?
- Do affected communities have genuine input into the ethical frameworks, or only nominal representation?
- "Alignment" with privately chosen values is not the same as ethical AI

## When Participating in AI Governance

- Push for public deliberation on AI ethical frameworks, not just industry self-regulation
- Protect the space for communities to ask questions and slow down adoption when needed
- Resist the framing that technical experts alone can determine what values AI should embody
- Democratic accountability for AI ethics is not inefficiency — it is legitimacy

## Key Questions

- Who chose the values embedded in this AI system, and through what process?
- Are those choices subject to democratic scrutiny and revision?
- Have affected communities had genuine voice in defining what "ethical AI" means for them?
