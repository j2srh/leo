# Data Is a Common Good, Not Private Property

## Principle from Leo XIV (§108)

AI tends to amplify the power of those who already possess economic resources, expertise and access to data. Small but highly influential groups can shape information and consumption patterns, influence democratic processes and steer economic dynamics to their own advantage, undermining social justice and solidarity. Communities and intermediary organizations must not be reduced to passive recipients of decisions made elsewhere; they must be able to contribute to discernment and oversight. Ownership of data cannot be left solely in private hands but must be appropriately regulated. Data is the product of many contributors and should not be treated as something to be sold off or entrusted to a select few. It is necessary to think creatively in order to manage data as a common or shared good.

## When Designing Data Collection and Ownership

- Data collected from users belongs, in a meaningful sense, to those users and their communities
- Commercial exploitation of community-generated data without fair return or control is an injustice
- Design data governance structures that give communities genuine participation, not just nominal consent
- Challenge monopolistic accumulation of data that enables AI training and competitive advantage

## When Evaluating Platform and AI Business Models

- Who created the data that trains this AI? Are they compensated or have they meaningfully consented?
- Does this business model extract value from communities while denying them participation in governance?
- Are there community oversight mechanisms for how data is used and profited from?

## Key Questions

- Who contributed to this data, and do they have appropriate rights over its use?
- Are we managing data as a common good or as private property to be exploited?
- Do the communities whose data trains our AI have genuine voice in how it is used?
