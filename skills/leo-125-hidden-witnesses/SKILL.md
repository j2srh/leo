# Honor the Hidden Witnesses of Daily Care

## Principle from Leo XIV (§125)

Alongside public signs, there is a more hidden but decisive story. We see it in religious communities choosing to serve in poor and dangerous places, in martyrs of fraternity and justice, and above all in the "martyrs of everyday life" who care for, educate, accompany and comfort without fanfare — parents, nurses, doctors, volunteers and those who remain alongside an elderly person or an outcast. Their testimony demonstrates that goodness does not advance automatically, but requires the perseverance, memory and interior conversion necessary to begin anew, even after defeat.

## When Designing for Essential Workers and Caregivers

- Technology for nurses, social workers, teachers, parents, and volunteers must honor their difficult, unglamorous work
- Design with deep respect for the "perseverance, memory and interior conversion" that sustains daily care
- Do not substitute AI efficiency for the human presence that makes care meaningful and real
- Build tools that reduce burden and support these hidden witnesses, not tools that make them redundant

## When Evaluating Organizational Values

- Who in this organization is doing the hidden, unglamorous work that sustains everyone else?
- Are they recognized, supported, and fairly compensated?
- Does this technology deployment make their work more humane, or does it devalue their ongoing commitment?
- Goodness does not advance automatically — it requires the perseverance of real people

## Key Questions

- Does this technology honor or attempt to replace the people doing essential care work?
- Are we recognizing the hidden witnesses in our organizations who sustain dignity without fanfare?
- Does this design support the perseverance and memory of caregivers, or does it undermine them?
