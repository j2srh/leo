# AI Decisions Touch Rights and Lives, Not Just Efficiency

## Principle from Leo XIV (§102)

The use of AI is never a purely technical matter: when it enters processes that affect people's lives, it touches on rights, opportunities, status and freedom. Important decisions — concerning employment, credit, access to public services or even a person's reputation — risk being fully delegated to automated systems that do not know "compassion, mercy, forgiveness, and above all, the hope that people are able to change." There is also a subtler danger: when AI systems present themselves as neutral and objective, they reflect and reinforce the stereotypes or ideological bias of their designers.

## When Designing Decision-Support or Automated Decision Systems

- No consequential decision about a person's life should be fully automated without human review
- Build explicit channels for compassion, exception, and appeal into every automated decision system
- "The algorithm decided" must never be a final answer to someone whose livelihood, freedom, or dignity is at stake
- Test for and disclose bias; "neutral" is not a technical property — it is a moral claim requiring evidence

## When Reviewing Automated Systems in Production

- Who currently has no mechanism to appeal or contest algorithmic decisions about them?
- Are there cases where the system cannot account for "the hope that people are able to change"?
- Are bias audits performed regularly, by independent parties with real authority?

## Key Questions

- Does this automated decision system have a compassionate human exception process?
- Who cannot appeal a decision made by this system, and why?
- Are we acknowledging and actively correcting the biases embedded in our training data and model design?
