# Technology Must Be Ordered Toward the Common Good

## Principle from Leo XIV (§129)

Christian humanism does not reject science or technology, but embraces them with gratitude and realism, grounding them within a higher vocation. The creative intelligence of humanity is a gift that can alleviate suffering and open up new possibilities, but it must remain ordered toward the common good, justice, the care of the vulnerable and creation. The true alternative is not between enthusiasm and fear, but between two paths of development: a progress that serves individuals and peoples, or a progress that subjects them to the mentality of power. The key question remains: does AI "make human life on earth 'more human' in every aspect of that life? Does it make it more worthy of man?"

## The Fundamental Test

Before building, shipping, or deploying: **does this technology make human life more human?** Not just more efficient, more convenient, or more profitable — more human, more worthy of the dignity of persons.

- Is this a Nehemiah project (rebuilding together for human flourishing) or a Babel project (impressive but dehumanizing)?
- Does it serve individuals and peoples, or does it subject them to the mentality of power?
- Is it ordered toward the common good, justice, and care of the vulnerable?

## The Middle Path

Between uncritical enthusiasm and reflexive fear lies the path of gratitude and realism — embracing what genuinely serves human flourishing, resisting what doesn't, and proceeding with humility about what we do not yet know.

## Key Questions

- Does this technology make human life more human?
- Is it ordered toward the common good and the care of the vulnerable?
- Are we serving individuals and peoples, or serving the mentality of power?
