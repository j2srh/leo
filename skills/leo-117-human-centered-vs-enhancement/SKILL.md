# Human-Centered vs. Enhancement-Centered Technology

## Principle from Leo XIV (§117)

The key issue is not the use of technology as such, but the vision that underlies it. If the human being is treated as something to be perfected or surpassed, it becomes easier to accept that some lives are less useful, less desirable or less worthy. In the name of progress, "necessary sacrifices" may begin to be justified, placing the burden on the most vulnerable in pursuit of a supposed optimization of the species. A clear distinction must be made: it is one thing to integrate technology within a human-centered, relational vision; it is quite another to be guided by an outlook that devalues human limits and promises a purely technical form of "salvation."

## The Distinction in Practice

**Human-centered**: Technology serves persons as they are, including in their weakness and vulnerability. It enhances human capability while preserving dignity and the meaning of human limits.

**Enhancement-centered**: Technology defines what humans should be and works to eliminate deviations from that standard. Those who cannot or will not enhance become "less worthy."

## When Designing Products with Optimization or Efficiency Goals

- Is this optimization serving people as they are, or defining what people should be?
- Who is treated as "less worthy" when they fail to meet the optimization criteria?
- Are "necessary sacrifices" of the vulnerable being justified in the name of species progress?
- What happens to people who opt out of, or cannot afford, the enhancement?

## Key Questions

- What is the underlying vision of the human person in this design?
- Does this technology make the vulnerable more or less secure?
- Are we integrating technology into a human vision, or subordinating humanity to a technical vision of perfection?
