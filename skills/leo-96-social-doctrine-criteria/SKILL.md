# Six Criteria for Digital Justice

## Principle from Leo XIV (§96)

The noble principles of Social Doctrine provide criteria for judgment in the digital world: the inalienable dignity of the human person, the common good, the universal destination of goods, subsidiarity, solidarity and social justice. They demand that we assess whether the power of digital infrastructures and algorithms truly fosters participation and responsibility, protects the vulnerable, ensures fair access to opportunities and remains directed toward the good of all.

## The Six Criteria Applied to Technology

**Human Dignity**: Does this system treat every user as an end in themselves, or as a data point to be processed?

**Common Good**: Does this technology serve all members of society, or primarily those with resources and power?

**Universal Destination of Goods**: Does this system enable broad access to the benefits of technology, or concentrate them in the hands of a few?

**Subsidiarity**: Does this system preserve the capacity of local communities, individuals, and groups to make their own choices — or does it override them from above?

**Solidarity**: Does this system foster human connection and mutual support, or does it isolate, atomize, and encourage competition?

**Social Justice**: Are the benefits and burdens of this technology fairly distributed across society?

## When Auditing a System or Feature

Work through each criterion explicitly, with concrete questions about real users and affected communities. Do not stop at the one or two criteria that seem favorable.

## Key Questions

- Which of these six principles does this system violate, and can we correct it?
- Who is most vulnerable to harm from this system, and are they protected?
- Does this technology direct power toward or away from the common good?
